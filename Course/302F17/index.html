Algorithms and Complexity

Goals

Learning Goals

  • Apply standard algorithmic paradigms to create, analyze runtime of, and prove the correctness of algorithms for common, real-world applications.
  • Appreciation of the creativity and beauty involved in algorithm design.
  • Appreciation of the connection between algorithms and data structures.
  • Awareness of the limits of efficient algorithms.

Learning Objectives

After completing this course, students should be able to

  • Describe the following paradigms, give examples of algorithms that employ the following paradigms, classify algorithms according to the following paradigms, and create algorithms using the following paradigms:
    • Divide and Conquer
    • Dynammic Programming
    • Greedy
    • Randomized
    • Local Search
  • Prove correctness of deterministic algorithms using inductive proofs, loop invariants, exchange arguments, and proofs by contradiction.
  • Analyze the runtime of algorithms. Analyze expected runtime of randomized algorithms using linearity of expectation and indicator variables.
  • Explain major graph algorithms and their analyses, and create new graph algorithms.
  • Give examples of algorithms that use data structures, and explain how the choice of data structure can affect the performance of an algorithm.
  • Understand the meaning of NP completeness, and describe several NP Complete problems.
  • Create a reduction from one problem to another.

Growth Mindset:
I expect all students to work to develop a growth mindset: awareness that intelligence is not fixed, but can increase through practice. I will provide many low-stakes opportunities for you to practice skills before your learning is assessed through quizzes and tests. Low-stakes practice opportunities include in-class active learning exercises and problem sets. In-class activities are graded not for correctness, but for participation, according to this rubric. While the problem sets are graded based on correctness, they account for a small portion of your total grade. (The primary purpose of having a grade is to encourage you to do them.) Furthermore, half of each homework grade is based on a self-grade and reflection, which is independent of the correctness of you homework. This self-grade and reflection will give you an opportunity to recognize mistakes and identify areas for further practice as needed.

Part of developing a growth mindeset is viewing critical feedback (through grades or other forms of assessment) for what it is: an opportunity to understand your weaknesses and improve on them, rather than a sign of failure. This perspective will not only tend to improve your grades, but will make learning more enjoyable.

Group Work:
Students will be assigned to small groups. You will work with your group in class to solve and discuss problems. Groups will be assigned randomly. Diverse groups tend to perform better than homogeneous groups, so please think about what diverse perspectives or background you might bring to your group. Your participation will be graded according to this rubric.

Attendance:
Because of the emphasis on group work, it is important that you attend class regularly. I will use responses to Plicker questions to determine attendance. For each unexcused absence after the first, you will receive a 3.33% deduction from your final grade.

Technology in the Classroom
You may NOT use any type of cell phone, tablet, or computer during class (except for cases of academic accommodation). Taking notes on a laptop has been shown to be less effective and lower quiz scores not only for the student using the device, but for nearby students as well.

Problem Sets
As mentioned above, problem sets are an opportunity for you to practice the skills of this course, and to become aware of areas for further practice and improvement. Because of this, only a small part of your grade is based on the correctness of your responses.

Each problem set will have two deadlines: (1) a problem set deadline and (2) self-grade/reflection deadline. Part 1 is your (attempted) solutions to the problem set. These must be submitted electronically in pdf form via Canvas by the start of class on the day the assignment is due. If done by hand, you should use a scanner to create a pdf version to submit. Retain the hard copy of Part 1, which you will need for the self-grade.

The problem set solutions will be posted soon after class on the day the homework is due. You should use these solutions to complete Part 2 of the assignment. Part 2 should be turned in as a paper copy at the beginning of class on the day it is due. Part 2 consists of

  • Self-Grade: Marked up problem set showing points earned for each problem, (along with errors marked) based on this rubric.
  • Reflection: A paragraph or two reflecting on your learning process while solving and grading the problem set. See TODOthis sheet for inspiration/instructions.

50% of your homework grade will be based on the correctness of Part 1 and 50% of your homework grade will be based on the quality of Part 2. The grade for Part 1 will primarily come from your self-grade (which we will spot check), and the grade for Part 2 will be based on this rubric.

It is very important that you submit Part 1 on time, because I will be posting solutions soon after the deadline, so that you can complete Part 2. Late Part 1 submissions will receive zero credit for Part 1. However, you may still recieve full credit for Part 2 even if Part 1 is late. Late Part 2 submissions will receive 50% credit for Part 2 if turned in before 5pm on the day they are due. Otherwise, you will receive no credit for Part 2, but will recieve credit for Part 1 (based on your self-grade) whenever you turn in Part 2. If homework is late for an excused reason, see below, accomodation will be made.

If you find during the self-grade that you have made a serious mistake, I recommend looking at the solution, then putting the solution away and trying to solve the problem again. This practice will help you to learn. If you find that you need more practice, try looking for relevant problems in the textbooks listed above, or ask me for suggestions.

Think of your homework as an essay. Just as you should not turn in a first draft of an essay for an assignment, you should not turn in a first draft of your homework. Writing good proofs/pseudocode/etc takes multiple revisions. While you may turn in (scanned) hand-written homework, typing your homework will make it easier to revise. I recommend LaTex, but it is not required. See these tutorials for a good introduction to LaTex.

You will not receive credit for homework that is illegible.

If you find a mistake in my solutions, I will give you bonus points worth the amount that would have been taken off for my error.

Honor Code:
You are expected to maintain the highest level of academic integrity in this course. This means that work you submit in CS302 must be the result of your own individual effort. You may discuss homework problems, general proof strategies, or algorithms with other students in the course, but you may not collaborate in the actual writing of problem sets. Each student must write up his or her own solutions, and may not read or copy another's solutions or solutions from previous semesters. This implies that one student should never have in her or his possession a copy of all or part of another student's homework. It is your own responsibility to protect your work from unauthorized access.

The exams and quizzes must be entirely your own work. You may not share any information about tests or quizzes with any other students, either within or outside of the course.

For those who plan to work on homework sets with others, I suggest the following procedure: spend as much time as you need working with others to understand the problems, take a break to do something else, and then go back and write up your solutions without the notes you used while working with the others. This will help ensure that you follow both the letter and the spirit of the honor code.
