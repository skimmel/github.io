Algorithms and Complexity

Goals

Learning Goals

  • Apply standard algorithmic paradigms to create, analyze the runtime of, and prove correctness of algorithms for common, real-world applications.
  • Appreciation of the creativity and beauty involved in algorithm design.
  • Appreciation of the connection between algorithms and data structures

Learning Objectives

After completing this course, students should be able to

  • Describe the following paradigms, give examples of algorithms that employ the following paradigms, classify algorithms according to the following paradigms, and create algorithms using the following paradigms:
    • Divide and Conquer
    • Dynammic Programming
    • Greedy
    • Randomized
    • Local Search
  • Prove correctness of deterministic algorithms using inductive proofs, loop invariants, exchange arguments, and proofs by contradiction.
  • Analyze the runtime of algorithms. Analyze expected runtime of randomized algorithms using linearity of expectation and indicator variables.
  • Explain major graph algorithms and their analyses, and create new graph algorithms.
  • Give examples of algorithms that use data structures, and explain how the choice of data structure can affect the performance of an algorithm.

Growth Mindset:
I expect all students to work to develop a growth mindset: awareness that intelligence is not fixed, but can increase through practice. Students will strive to see critical feedback (through grades or other forms of assessment) for what it is: an opportunity to understand your weaknesses and improve on them, rather than a sign of failure. Developing such a mindset will not only tend to improve your grades, but will make learning more enjoyable.

Group Work:
Students will be assigned to groups of three. You will work with your group in class to solve and discuss problems. Groups will be assigned randomly. Diverse groups tend to perform better than homogeneous groups, so please think about what diverse perspectives or background you might bring to your group. You will be graded according to the following rubric \url{http://shelbykimmel.com/Documents/Teaching/GroupWorkRubric.pdf}.

Attendance:
Because of the emphasis on group work, it is important that you attend class regularly. I will use responses to Plicker questions to determine attendance. For each unexcused absence after the first, you will receive a 3.33% deduction in your final grade.

Technology in the Classroom
You may NOT use any type of cell phone, tablet, or computer during class (except for cases of academic accommodation). Taking notes on a laptop has been shown to be less effective and lower quiz scores not only for the student using technology, but for nearby students as well.

Problem Sets
Each problem set will have two deadlines associated with it, a problem set deadline and self-grade/reflection deadline:

  1. Problem set due to submission server, which closes at the beginning of the class period on the day it is due.
  2. Self-grade and reflection due to submission server, which closes at the beginning of the class period on the day it is due.
    • Self-Grade: Marked up problem set showing points for each problem, (along with errors marked) based on this rubric. Your points on this part will make up 50% of your grade for the problem set. We will double check your grading.
    • Reflection.
      • A one sentence reflection for each problem describing why you think it was assigned, and putting the problem into the context of the broader goals of the course.
      • A paragraph reflecting on the learning process for you in the course of both solving and grading the problem set.
      The reflection will be worth 50% of your grade for the problem set. We will grade based this rubric.

Think of your homework as an essay. Just as you should not turn in a first draft of an essay for an assignment, you should not turn in a first draft of your homework. Writing good proofs/pseudocode/etc takes multiple revisions. While you may turn in hand-written homework, typing your homework will make it easier to revise. I recommend LaTex, but it is not required. You will not receive credit for homework that is illegible.

There will be no late homework accepted. If you have an approved reason (see below) for missing a deadline, accommodations will be made.

If you find a mistake in my solution, I will give you bonus points worth the amount that would have been taken off based on the corresponding rubric.

When you find during the self grade that you have made a mistake, I recommend looking at the solution, then putting the solution away, and solving the problem again. This practice will help to train your brain so approach problems correctly.

Honor Code:
You are expected to maintain the highest level of academic integrity in this course. This means that work you submit in CS200 must be the result of your own individual effort. You may discuss homework problems, general proof strategies or algorithms with other students in the course, but you may not collaborate in the actual writing of problem sets. Each student must write up his or her own solutions, and may not read or copy another's solutions or solutions from previous semesters. This implies that one student should never have in her or his possession a copy of all or part of another student's homework. It is your own responsibility to protect your work from unauthorized access.

The exams and quizzes must be entirely your own work.

For those who plan to work on homework sets with others, I suggest the following procedure: spend as much time as you need working with others to understand the problems, take a break to do something else, and then go back and write up your solutions without the notes you used while working with the others. This will help ensure that you follow both the letter and the spirit of the honor code.

Excused Absences:
